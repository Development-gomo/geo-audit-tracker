The headline

Five lines, if it has to compress to five.

Each is a quantified claim drawn from the cuts that follow.

01

On comparison prompts, the three LLMs agree on which brands to recommend 73% of the time. They cite almost entirely different sources to justify it.

02

Track B — rewrite content to be cited

The evidence chain has broken away from search.

Ranking in Google and being cited by an LLM turn out to be different games. The format that gets cited is not the format most brand sites publish.

The pages LLMs cite are largely invisible in Google.

Of the 500 most-cited URLs, 93% have zero tracked organic keywords. Only 5% rank in the top three for anything. The median URL-Rating of the top 100 is zero.

Ranking well in Google does not get you cited. Being cited does not require ranking. Optimising one does not deliver the other.

V2 — Pass. Only 5.2% of the no-keyword URLs are German or Swedish-language pages. Non-English coverage is not driving the result.

The format that gets cited is comparison, not corporate.

Among the top 100 cited URLs, 20% are "vs" or "versus" pages and 19% are "best of" or "top N" lists. A further 18% of the top 500 are market-outlook and forecast pages — research firms such as GM Insights, Mordor Intelligence and Statista punch well above their weight. 18% match electrification, EV, battery or zero-emission keywords in the URL.

LLMs preferentially pull from explicit comparison and quantified outlook content. It is the opposite of the brand-narrative prose most OEM sites publish.

Brands are talked about far more than their own sites are cited.

Across tracked manufacturers on unbranded prompts, brands are named in response text roughly seven times as often as their own domain is cited. Median self-source ratio is 0.105; aggregate 0.146. An owned site appears for about one in ten of the times a brand is discussed.

Owned-media investment converts into AI visibility only indirectly, through third parties who cite or discuss the brand.

V3 — Revised. An earlier "~14×" figure used mismatched bases (8 runs of mentions against 6 days of citations). With aligned bases the multiplier is ~7×. Direction unchanged.

When LLMs agree on the recommendation, they agree on the brand — not the evidence.

Compared engine-to-engine on the same prompt and market, mean agreement on the brand set is 0.41. Mean agreement on the URL set is 0.035. A twelve-fold gap. On branded comparison prompts, 73% of engine pairs name the same brands while citing essentially disjoint URLs. Median pairwise source overlap between any two engines is 0.074.

There is no single source of record. The conclusion is stable. The evidence chain behind it is contested and substitutable.

Resale marketplaces outrank manufacturer websites as sources.

Of the top 100 most-cited URLs, 23 are used-equipment and aggregator pages — truck1.eu, used-machinery marketplaces, dealer aggregators. Seven are manufacturer-owned.

The highest-value citation real estate sits with third-party commercial sites, not OEM .coms.

Reddit is a real source. On one engine only.

Forum and Reddit threads are 6.2% of top-cited URLs. SearchGPT accounts for the overwhelming majority. Copilot and AI Overview rarely cite Reddit, even on identical prompts. YouTube, separately, is one of the single largest sources overall.

Community-channel investment matters, but it pays off very differently across engines.

Some brands are named but almost never linked.

When BYD, John Deere, JCB or Tesla Semi are named in a response, a link to the brand is included fewer than one time in seven: BYD 8%, John Deere 10%, JCB 13%, Tesla Semi 13%. At the other end of the scale, Yanmar, Caterpillar, Yutong, Volvo Buses and Volvo CE are linked 59–67% of the time they are named.

Narrative presence and citation presence are separate currencies. They diverge sharply by brand.

Track A — shift KPI to AI visibility

A single LLM run is not "the answer".

The strongest argument for continuous, multi-engine measurement. The engines disagree with each other. They also disagree with themselves.

The lead recommendation is unstable from one run to the next.

Firing the same prompt repeatedly, the top recommended brand changes at least once in 58% of unbranded cases. SearchGPT highest at 65%. AI Overview lowest at 53%.

AI visibility can only be measured as a distribution over repeated, tracked observations. One observation is not a measurement.

V1 — Reframed. The eight repeated runs came from a single 44-minute export window, not eight separate days. This is the model's own sampling stochasticity under fixed input, not day-to-day drift. With the model and the web both unchanged, the answer still moves 58% of the time.

The engines differ in volume more than in volatility.

AI Overview is the most stable on the response layer: top brand holds in 47% of repeated runs, against Copilot at 43% and SearchGPT at 35%. On citations, Copilot returns far fewer per prompt — median 29 against SearchGPT's 90 and AI Overview's 67.

No single engine is a safe proxy for the others. If only one can be monitored as a baseline, AI Overview is the most reliable.

V5 — Reframed. An earlier read of "Copilot citations 2× more volatile" was largely a volume artifact. Once citation volume is equalised, Copilot is no worse and on some metrics the most stable. The robust finding is the volume gap, not a stability gap.
Cross-cutting

Brand narrative is market-specific. The plumbing is not.

The leading brand changes by country. The source mix does not.

On unbranded prompts, the top brand is Scania in Sweden (18.4%), Freightliner in the US (9.2%) and Mercedes-Benz in Germany (16.0%). The citation source-category mix is structurally identical across all three markets. Largest per-category spread: 3.7 percentage points.

The kinds of sources LLMs trust are globally homogeneous. What differs by market is which brands those sources elevate.

One competitor dominates narrative share.

Mercedes-Benz is named 342 times across 99 comparative prompts. More than any other brand in any other intent. On branded comparisons — where users name brands — Mercedes-Benz still leads at 72 mentions, despite being explicitly named in only about a fifth of prompts.

Narrative share concentrates heavily on a few incumbents, named or not. The top five brands account for 43% of all mentions.

V4 — Pass. A 25-response audit found 100% of Mercedes-Benz mentions in commercial-vehicle context. No passenger-car drift.

LLMs name four times more brands when comparing than when explaining.

Average brands named per response, by intent: comparative and comparison prompts ~4.2. Branded comparison 3.2. Recommendation 1.7. Informational, support and technical ~1.1. Source mix shifts too — informational prompts ("how does X work") pull only 6% manufacturer-owned citations. Action and transactional prompts ("find a dealer") jump to 22%.

Owned content is least visible exactly at the awareness stage.

Counterweight

What is not happening.

Three common assumptions the data does not support.

Press releases barely register.

Just 2.4% of top-cited URLs match newsroom or press-release patterns. PR distribution is not a meaningful AI-visibility lever in this category.

Wikipedia is under 1%.

Only 2 of the top 100 cited URLs are Wikipedia pages. Far below the common "AI cites Wikipedia" assumption.

AI Overview selectively refuses a specific set of prompts.

Thirteen prompts get persistent no-render across all three markets. They cluster on informational and decision-stage queries. Branded prompts are under-represented in the refusal set. AIO's silence is patterned, not random.

Naming a brand anchors which brands get discussed. It does not anchor which sources get cited. The brand list converges. The evidence chain does not.

Scope

What this evidence covers, and what it does not.

Response and citation data can see how brands are surfaced and sourced. It cannot see agent transactions or commerce surfaces.
